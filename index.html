Is your direct booking site ready for AI?

This audit checks whether an assistant can find, recommend and book you.

1. The raw text a site sends first.

This is the version an AI reads. It is the site stripped of its looks, just the words and the structure underneath.

2. The hidden labels.

A tidy block of information tucked into the page that spells out plainly what the place is, what it costs, and where it is. AI relies on these, because it does not have to guess. This is what mostly decides whether AI can find and recommend you.

3. The stuff that loads late.

Some sites only show the price and the calendar after extra code runs. That late content is often invisible to AI, because AI usually sees only the first raw version. This is what decides whether AI can actually book you.

The two scores below follow from this. Visibility is about the first two layers. Bookability is about whether your prices and booking sit in the raw version or load too late.

Your website

Your address is sent to a server to fetch the page. Nothing else is sent or stored.

This one check is different from the rest of the tool. To read your site, the address you enter is sent to a small server that fetches the page. Nothing else about you is sent or stored. The rest of the audit still runs entirely in your browser.

What we measured

Change any answer if the check missed something on your page.

Can a guest get from choosing dates to a confirmed booking without a step only a human can complete?
An assistant needs a concrete path it can work through or hand a guest to. JavaScript-heavy steps and widgets still count if they block that path.
2
Your listing copy
Does the description lead with what the place is and who it suits, rather than opening with a list of amenities?
An AI summarises the opening. A feature-first opening gives it nothing to recommend on.
Does the copy state concrete facts (location context, capacity, key amenities, distances) in clear sentences rather than marketing adjectives?
"Cosy and charming" is invisible to a machine. "Sleeps four, ten minutes from the station" is not.
Is the cancellation and booking policy stated in the text itself?
It is one of the first things an assistant checks before recommending.
Does your site have structured data markup (schema.org) describing the property and rooms?
This is how an AI understands what you are without guessing.
Is your core content (what the place is, where it is, what makes it suitable) in plain crawlable text rather than locked inside images or a graphic?
If it is only in a photo or design, the machine does not have it. The copy should also make sense on its own without relying on visuals to fill gaps.
Does your site directly answer the natural questions a guest asks an assistant: is it suitable for families, is there parking, how far to the centre, what is the cancellation policy?
AI recommends the sites that actually answer the question being asked.
Are your prices and live availability visible as text on the page, rather than only inside a booking widget or calendar that loads separately?
An agent cannot read what is locked in an embedded widget.
Is the essential booking information reachable in the first HTML response, rather than only after the page fully renders in a browser?
Agents often see the initial HTML, not the rendered page.
Does your site have structured data for prices and availability specifically (offer / price schema), not just a general property description?
Property markup helps an assistant find you. Offer and price markup helps it know what it can book.
