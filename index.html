Is your direct booking site ready for AI?

This audit checks whether an assistant can read and understand your website well enough to recommend and book it. Answer ten short questions about your site and listing copy.

Section A — Your website
Does your website have structured data markup (schema.org — e.g. for your property, rooms, and prices)?
This is how an AI reads what you offer without guessing. It is the single strongest machine-readability signal.
Are your prices and availability visible as readable text on the page, rather than only inside an embedded booking widget or calendar that loads separately?
An AI often cannot read what is locked inside a third-party widget.
Can the core facts — what the place is, where it is, what it costs, how to book — be reached in the page's text without requiring JavaScript or several clicks?
Machines frequently see the first HTML response, not the fully-rendered page.
Does your site answer, in plain text, the natural questions a guest would ask an assistant (suitable for families, parking, distance to the centre, cancellation policy)?
AI recommends the sites that actually answer the question being asked.
Is there a clear, stable booking path with a real linkable URL an agent could point a guest to, rather than a flow buried behind a pop-up or third-party redirect?
An assistant needs somewhere concrete to send the guest.
Section B — Your listing copy
Does the description lead with WHO the place is for and WHY they would come, rather than opening with a list of amenities?
An AI summarises the opening. A feature-first opening gives it nothing to recommend on.
Does the copy state concrete facts — location context, capacity, key amenities, distances — in clear sentences rather than marketing adjectives?
"Cosy and charming" is invisible to a machine. "Sleeps four, ten minutes from the station" is not.
Is the cancellation and booking policy stated in the text itself?
It is one of the first things an assistant checks before recommending.
Are your key facts in the text, rather than buried in images, PDFs, or graphics an AI cannot read?
If it is only in a photo, the machine does not have it.
Does the copy make sense on its own, without relying on the photos or page design to fill the gaps?
An AI often has only the words.
